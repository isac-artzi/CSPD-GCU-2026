2. What is AI, Really?

Let's get one thing out of the way: AI is not magic, and it is not alive. At its core, artificial intelligence is software that finds patterns in data and uses those patterns to make predictions. That's it. Everything else — the hype, the fear, the sci-fi fantasies — is built on top of that simple foundation.

Think about how you learned to predict what comes next in a sentence. If I say "The cat sat on the ___," you immediately think "mat" or "chair" or "couch." You can do that because you've read thousands of sentences and absorbed patterns of how English works. Modern AI does something remarkably similar, except it has read billions of pages and can draw on all of them at once.

The one-sentence definition

Artificial intelligence is software that learns patterns from data and uses those patterns to make predictions or decisions. No consciousness. No feelings. No hidden agenda. Just very sophisticated pattern matching.

A Brief History: How We Got Here

1950s – 1980s Rule-Based Systems (Expert Systems)

Humans wrote explicit if/then rules by hand. "If the patient has a fever AND a cough, suggest flu." Useful but brittle — they could only handle situations the programmers anticipated.

1990s – 2000s Machine Learning

Instead of writing rules, we gave the computer data and let it find the rules itself. Spam filters are a classic example: show the system 10,000 spam emails and 10,000 real emails, and it figures out what makes spam "spammy."

2010s Deep Learning

Neural networks with many layers could learn incredibly complex patterns — recognizing faces in photos, translating languages, even beating humans at Go. Required enormous amounts of data and computing power.

2020 – 2023 Large Language Models (LLMs)

GPT, Claude, Gemini — models trained on most of the text on the internet. They can write essays, explain calculus, draft emails, and hold conversations. This is where ChatGPT comes from.

2024 – Present Agentic AI

The frontier today. AI systems that don't just answer questions — they take actions. They can search the web, write and run code, manage files, and complete multi-step tasks autonomously. This is what we're here to learn about.

Why this matters for teachers

Understanding this timeline helps you see that AI isn't one thing — it's a spectrum. The AI in your email spam filter is very different from the AI in ChatGPT, and both are different from the agentic AI we'll build today. Knowing the differences helps you make informed decisions about which tools to use in your classroom and how to teach students about them.

3. Meet the Large Language Model (LLM)

You've almost certainly used one already. ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Copilot — these are all built on large language models. Let's pull back the curtain on what they actually are.

How LLMs Work (The Non-Technical Version)

An LLM is trained by reading a massive amount of text — books, websites, academic papers, forums, code, and more. During training, it plays a game millions of times: given a sequence of words, predict the next word. Over time, it gets extraordinarily good at this game.

When you type a prompt into ChatGPT, the model isn't "thinking" about your question. It's generating one word (technically, one token) at a time, each time asking itself: "Given everything that's come before, what word is most likely to come next?" The result often looks like understanding, but it's really very sophisticated pattern completion.

The analogy: Your world's most well-read writing partner

Imagine a writing partner who has read every textbook, every novel, every scientific paper, every coding tutorial, and every Reddit thread ever posted — but has never lived in the real world. They can write beautifully about how rain feels on your skin because they've read a million descriptions of it, not because they've ever been wet. That's an LLM. Incredibly knowledgeable, but knowledge without experience.

Three Key Properties You Need to Know

Property What It Means Why It Matters
Context Window The amount of text the model can "see" at once — both your input and its output. Measured in tokens (roughly ¾ of a word). If your conversation is too long, the model "forgets" the beginning. Modern models have windows of 100K+ tokens, but there are still limits.
Temperature A setting that controls how "creative" vs. "predictable" the output is. Low temperature = safe, expected answers. High temperature = surprising, varied answers. For a math tutor, you want low temperature (consistent, accurate). For a creative writing helper, you might want higher temperature.
Tokens The basic unit the model reads and writes. A token is roughly ¾ of an English word. "Photosynthesis" = 4 tokens. "Cat" = 1 token. API pricing is based on tokens. Understanding this helps you estimate costs and design efficient prompts.
LLMs can be confidently wrong

Because LLMs are pattern-completion machines (not fact-lookup engines), they can generate text that sounds authoritative but is factually incorrect. This is called a hallucination. An LLM might confidently tell you that a made-up research paper exists, complete with fake authors and a fake journal. Always verify important claims independently. This is one of the key things to teach your students as well.

4. From Chatbot to Agent: The Big Leap

Here's the most important concept we'll cover today. There's a fundamental difference between a chatbot and an AI agent, and understanding that difference is what this entire workshop is about.

A chatbot is like a reference librarian. You ask a question, you get an answer. The librarian doesn't follow you home, organize your bookshelf, or check whether you actually read the book. They answer your question and wait for the next one.

An agent is like a teaching assistant. You say, "Help my students review for the chemistry exam," and the TA figures out the steps: review the syllabus, identify key topics, create practice problems, grade the results, and report back on which concepts the class is struggling with. The TA plans, acts, evaluates, and adapts — without you specifying every step.

Side-by-Side Comparison

Capability Chatbot AI Agent
Interaction Style Reactive — waits for your question Proactive — can initiate actions on its own
Conversation Single-turn (one question, one answer) Multi-step (works through complex workflows)
Memory Limited or none between sessions Remembers context and learns from past interactions
Tools No tools — can only generate text Uses tools: searches the web, runs code, reads files, calls APIs
Planning No planning — responds to the immediate prompt Breaks big tasks into steps and executes them in order
Self-Correction None — if it's wrong, you must notice and re-prompt Evaluates its own output and tries again if the result isn't right

The Agent Loop

Every AI agent, regardless of how sophisticated it is, follows the same fundamental cycle. We call it the Agent Loop. The agent perceives its environment, makes a plan, takes action, observes the result, reflects on whether it worked, and then loops back to perceive the updated situation. This cycle repeats until the task is complete.

Agent
Loop
👁 Perceive
🧠 Plan
⚡ Act
🔍 Observe
💭 Reflect
The Agent Loop in Plain English
  1. Perceive — The agent reads the task description, user input, or current state of the world.
  2. Plan — It decides what to do next: which tool to use, what question to ask, what step comes first.
  3. Act — It takes that action: runs a web search, writes some text, calls an API, generates a quiz question.
  4. Observe — It looks at the result. Did the action work? Is the output correct? Was there an error?
  5. Reflect — It evaluates: "Is this good enough? Do I need to try again? Should I take a different approach?"

Then it loops back to Perceive with the updated information and continues until the task is done.

Why should teachers care about the agent loop?

Notice something familiar? The agent loop is essentially the scientific method (observe, hypothesize, experiment, analyze) and the engineering design process (define, ideate, prototype, test, refine). When you teach your students about AI agents, you can connect this directly to processes they already know.

5. What Makes AI "Agentic"?

Not every AI system is an agent. An AI becomes "agentic" when it possesses some combination of five key capabilities. The more of these pillars it has, the more autonomous and useful it becomes. Let's explore each one — with examples from your classroom.

🤖
(a) Autonomy
The agent acts without needing step-by-step human instruction. You give it a goal ("generate a review worksheet for Chapter 5"), and it figures out the steps on its own — reading the chapter topics, selecting question types, balancing difficulty levels, and formatting the output.
STEM Example: You tell the agent "Prepare a lab safety briefing for tomorrow's chemistry class." It identifies the experiment, looks up the relevant safety protocols, creates a pre-lab checklist, and drafts discussion questions — all from a single request.
🔧
(b) Tool Use
The agent can reach beyond text generation to use external tools: search the web for current information, run Python code to solve math problems, read files and documents, call external APIs, and even control other software.
STEM Example: A physics tutor agent that doesn't just explain projectile motion but actually runs the kinematic equations in Python, generates a plot of the trajectory, and shows the student a visual graph alongside the explanation.
📋
(c) Planning
The agent breaks complex tasks into manageable steps and executes them in a logical order. It can handle dependencies ("I need to do X before Y"), deal with contingencies ("If the student gets this wrong, try a different approach"), and manage multi-step workflows.
STEM Example: An engineering design review agent that first reads the student's design brief, then checks if constraints were met, then evaluates the solution's feasibility, then provides structured feedback — each step building on the previous one.
🧠
(d) Memory
The agent remembers context across interactions. It knows what a student struggled with last week, what topics have been covered in the course, and what approach worked best for a particular learner. This is different from a chatbot that starts fresh every conversation.
STEM Example: A math tutor that remembers a student consistently confuses area and perimeter. The next time it generates practice problems, it includes extra scaffolding on those specific concepts without the teacher having to re-explain the student's history.
🪞
(e) Reflection
The agent evaluates its own output and improves it. If it generates a quiz and notices one question is ambiguous, it rewrites it. If a student says "I don't understand," it tries a completely different explanation strategy rather than repeating itself.
STEM Example: A lab report feedback agent that drafts its feedback, then re-reads it checking for tone ("Is this constructive, not discouraging?"), accuracy ("Did I correctly identify the student's error?"), and actionability ("Can the student actually use this to improve?").
Not all-or-nothing

An AI system doesn't need all five pillars to be useful. The agent we build today will focus primarily on Autonomy, Tool Use, and Planning. Memory and Reflection are more advanced features that you can add as you grow more comfortable. Think of these pillars as a menu, not a checklist.

6. Agentic AI in Your STEM Classroom

Let's get concrete. Here are real use cases where an AI agent (not just a chatbot) can meaningfully support your teaching — without replacing your judgment, expertise, or the human connection that makes great teaching great.

🔬
Lab Report Feedback Agent

Reads student lab reports and provides structured feedback on scientific methodology: Was the hypothesis testable? Were variables properly controlled? Is the conclusion supported by the data? Gives constructive criticism that teaches scientific thinking, not just grammar corrections. The teacher reviews the feedback before sharing it with students.

🧮
Socratic Math Tutor

Guides students through problem-solving using the Socratic method — asking guiding questions instead of giving answers. If a student is stuck on a quadratic equation, the agent might ask, "What do you notice about the coefficients?" or "Can you identify a common factor?" It builds mathematical reasoning, not answer dependency.

⚙️
Design Review Bot

Evaluates student engineering notebooks and design proposals against a rubric you define. Checks for completeness (Did they document their constraints? Did they consider alternative solutions?), provides feedback on the engineering design process, and flags common mistakes — like jumping to a solution before properly defining the problem.

📝
Adaptive Quiz Generator

Creates quizzes tailored to your specific curriculum, standards, and difficulty level. Unlike generic quiz makers, an agentic version can adapt: if a student gets a question wrong, the next question targets the same concept at a simpler level. It exports clean formats ready for Google Forms or your LMS.

📚
Lesson Plan Assistant

Given your state standards, grade level, and time constraints, the agent drafts lesson plans complete with learning objectives (mapped to standards), warm-up activities, direct instruction outlines, guided practice ideas, and exit ticket questions. You review and modify — it handles the first draft so you can focus on refinement.

🎯
Differentiation Helper

Takes any assignment or reading and produces differentiated versions: a scaffolded version for students who need extra support, an on-level version, and an enriched version for students ready for a challenge. Maintains the same core learning objectives across all levels while adjusting vocabulary, complexity, and support structures.

8. Responsible AI in Education

Before we start building, we need to talk about responsibility. AI tools are powerful, and with that power comes an obligation to use them thoughtfully — especially when our students are involved.

This isn't about fear. It's about professionalism. Just as you would vet a new textbook or evaluate a guest speaker before bringing them into your classroom, you should approach AI tools with informed judgment.

  • Always review AI output before sharing with students. AI-generated content can contain errors, biases, or inappropriate material. You are the quality control. Treat AI output as a first draft that needs your expert review, never as a finished product.
  • Never input student PII into AI systems. Student names, grades, IDs, health information, behavioral records, IEP details — none of this belongs in a prompt to any AI system. Use anonymized data or fictional examples. Your school's FERPA obligations apply to AI tools just as they do to any other technology.
  • AI is your assistant, not a replacement for teacher judgment. No AI can replicate the relationships you build with students, the contextual knowledge you have about their lives, or the professional judgment you bring to your classroom. Use AI to handle routine tasks so you have more time for the irreplaceable human parts of teaching.
  • Teach students about AI limitations. Your students will use AI throughout their lives and careers. Teaching them about hallucination, bias, and the difference between correlation and understanding is one of the most valuable things you can do. Help them become critical consumers of AI, not passive users.
  • Know your school's AI policy — or help create one. Many districts are still developing their AI policies. Find out where yours stands. If no policy exists, that's an opportunity. The best policies are written with teacher input, not handed down from above. Your experience from today's workshop makes you a valuable voice in that conversation.
A note on bias

LLMs learn from internet text, which contains societal biases — around race, gender, socioeconomic status, and more. These biases can surface in AI output in subtle ways: defaulting to male pronouns for scientists, generating examples that assume certain cultural contexts, or producing recommendations that work better for some student populations than others. Be vigilant. If something feels off, it probably is.
